Behavior

How foxes get food

Foxes do a lot of hunting at dawn and dusk, but can hunt anytime. They hunt by stalking their live prey. They have excellent hearing and use a pouncing technique that allows them to kill the prey quickly.

​

How it protects itself from predators

Foxes are the smallest wild members of the canine family, which includes companion dogs, coyotes and wolves. They build small burrows or dens in grasslands to protect themselves from predators such as wolves. All foxes use a lot of patience and an exceptional sense of smell to catch their prey with a pounce.

​

Sleeping habits

They may share their dens with woodchucks or badgers. Unlike badgers, which fastidiously clean their earths and defecate in latrines, red foxes habitually leave pieces of prey around their dens. The average sleep time of a captive red fox is 9.8 hours per day.

Diet Information

Foxes are omnivores and eat small mammals, birds, reptiles, frogs, eggs, insects, worms, fish, crabs, mollusks, fruits, berries, vegetables, seeds, fungi and carrion. In winter they mainly eat mammals, such as mice, rabbits and other small animals.

Animal Communications

​

  • Posture
    • Foxes communicate with each other in a variety of ways. As in the domestic dog and many other carnivores, many different facial expressions and body postures are used. Friendly greetings such as tail wagging are used when meeting family members, and aggressive behaviour may be directed towards intruding neighbouring foxes.
  • Sound
    • Foxes are mostly silent animals but this does not mean they do not have a broad repertoire of sounds. A wide range of calls is used from cubs' small barks asking for their mother's attention, to warning calls, to barks and screams performed to locate another fox or a mate.
    • Calls are made all year around but most people hear foxes calling in winter, perhaps because there is less vegetation to muffle the sound.
  • Smell
    • Urine and faeces are used to communicate to other group members and to neighbouring foxes. For example, urine is used to mark their home range (the space where an animal lives) and both urine and faeces are used to signal some specific state, such as when females are fertile. The use of smell to communicate information to other animals is called scent marking. Faeces in particular are placed in conspicuous spots where they can easily be found by other foxes, such as on objects, paths, gates or even on food remains.
  • Interesting fact - We don't know for sure if foxes can recognize each other's smell, but the research done shows that foxes probably use urine and faeces to tell whether there are intruders in an area and therefore it is possible that they can also recognise the smell of familiar individuals. We do not understand the 'code' of scent marking but we know that there are seasonal differences in this code and that the scents of males and females differ.

Foxes in Pop Culture

Music:

​

The word foxy is referenced in music often in band, album, and song names. It doesn’t refer to a fox itself, but instead to young and beautiful women. Deriving from the ancient british word of Foxismonitism, meaning attractive or sexually appealing, it has evolved along the years to become a more well known, and someone slang word, foxy.

​

Books, Comics, Literature:

Many famous children's stories contain foxes as protagonist. These include The Gingerbread Man, Chicken Licken, The Sly Red Fox and the Little Red Hen and Fantastic Mr Fox. Such stories typify the stereotype that foxes are cunning and deceitful, but also successful and to be admired.
